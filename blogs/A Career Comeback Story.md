If you had told me a few years ago that I would be building careers for persons with disabilities in Bengaluru — working at an inclusive employment NGO, learning sign language, and researching employers the way I once researched pharmaceutical compounds — I would have smiled and said: "But I am a pharmacist." And yet, here I am at WinVinaya Foundation. Doing exactly that. And every day of it feels like purpose.  
I am Lavanya. I hold an M.Pharmacy in Pharmaceutics and worked as an R&D Executive — a world of precision, deep analysis, and relentless problem-solving. Then I stepped away after marriage. A pause that millions of women across India know deeply — one the world calls a "gap," but I now see as quiet preparation for something far greater.  
  
"A career break is not the end of your story. It is the space between two powerful chapters."  
  
  
WinVinaya Foundation: Inclusive Employment in Action  
Before I tell you my story, let me tell you where I work — because it matters.  
WinVinaya Foundation is a Bengaluru-based nonprofit that trains and places persons with disabilities and women from underserved communities into dignified, sustainable employment. In over 10 years of operation, the Foundation has trained 1,600+ individuals and placed 706+ people across sectors including manufacturing, logistics, retail, and services. In FY25–26 alone, we worked with 37 employer partners across 9 sectors — including organisations like TE Connectivity, DSV, and Schneider Electric — who believe that inclusive hiring is not charity. It is good business.  
I am proud to be part of this team.  
  
Starting Over After a Career Break: The Real Dilemma  
When I joined WinVinaya Foundation, many of my colleagues communicated through Indian Sign Language (ISL) — a language I had never learned. I remember asking myself: Can I truly connect here? Will I really belong? That uncertainty was real. And I am not ashamed to say it.  
Discomfort is not a sign that you are in the wrong place. Sometimes, it is the clearest sign that you are exactly where you need to grow.  
I did not step back. I leaned forward. Day by day, I began to learn the beauty of a language that needs no voice to be powerful. Today, I can communicate with my colleagues who use ISL — and I conduct placement classes for our candidates and students who use sign language, helping them prepare and step confidently into the workforce.  
"Inclusion is not just a value we preach. It is a practice we choose — every single day."  
  
How My Research Skills Power Disability Inclusion Placement Work  
In pharmaceutics, I was trained to go beyond the surface and never accept the first result. Those skills did not stay in the lab. They came with me — and they are more useful here than I ever imagined.  
Here are three skills from my pharmaceutical career that I use every single day in inclusive placement work:  
  
Deep research: I study employers the way I studied compounds — their culture, values, inclusion readiness, and track record with diverse talent. Surface-level research is never enough.  
Data-driven candidate mapping: I match candidates to roles using structured assessment, not guesswork. Every placement decision is evidence-based.  
Iterative problem-solving: Just like in R&D, I never stop at the first attempt. Every placement challenge is a formula I am determined to crack — one adjustment at a time.  
  
  
What Inclusive Placement Work Actually Looks Like  
People often ask me what I do on a typical day. Here is the honest answer:  
Mapping candidates to careers — beyond guesswork  
I assess each candidate's skills, communication strengths, learning pace, and aspirations. I then identify roles — across our 37 employer partners — where they will not just survive but thrive. Placement is not about filling a vacancy. It is about building a career.  
Building employer relationships that last  
Inclusive hiring requires trust. I work with employer partners to set expectations, provide onboarding support, and ensure that both the candidate and the organisation are set up for success. Many of our employer relationships span multiple hiring cycles because the placements hold.  
  
Is It Too Late to Return to Work After a Career Break?  
For every woman who has been told the workforce is not for her anymore — this section is for you.  
The answer is no. It is not too late. Here is what I have learned:  
  
Your skills do not expire. Research, analysis, precision, empathy — these travel with you regardless of how long you were away.  
Your gap is not a liability. It is lived experience. In my case, the pause gave me perspective I could not have gained in a laboratory.  
The right organisation will not see a break — it will see a person. WinVinaya Foundation hired me not despite my career break, but with full knowledge of it.  
Discomfort at re-entry is normal. Not knowing ISL on day one was not a failure. It was day one.  
Start. The rest aligns. You do not need to have all the answers before you walk through the door.  
  
"Your field may change. Your skills will follow you everywhere. Start — the rest will align."
